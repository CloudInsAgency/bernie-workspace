# Marketing Creative — The Hyperdopamine Ad Framework

This skill encodes the Sabri Suby / King Kong methodology Craig adopted as
his standard playbook across all projects (CloudPriceDeals, Cloud Insurance,
Chris Burns Realtor, PollPulse, WigOut, ZeroSugar, Cloud Dispatch Ops,
Cloud Design Studio).

**Use it whenever generating any marketing asset.** Read this skill before
writing creative; pull project-specific details (dream buyer, voice, brand
colors, claims you're allowed to make) from the project's CLAUDE.md.

---

## The Core Thesis: People Beat Algorithms

Sabri's central claim, repeated throughout: **stop obsessing over targeting
and account structure; obsess over human psychology and copy.** A great media
buyer with a weak ad gets slaughtered by a beginner with a dynamite ad.

Concretely: ~80% of performance comes from creative; ~20% from settings.
Don't agonize over interest stacks, lookalike percentages, or bid caps until
the creative is dialed.

---

## What Drives Engagement: Follow Facebook's Own Data

Every year Facebook publishes its "Most Widely Viewed Content Report." The
top pages are always the same category: **news and gossip** (Lad Bible, Unilad,
ENews, People, TMZ). That's what the platform's own users gravitate to.

> "Do not hate the player, hate the game. We're not going to change human
> psychology. We want to tap into that jet stream and make our business fly
> along with the existing behavior."

**This means our ads must do two jobs:**

1. **Deliver news, value, or findings** — that's what people click on
2. **Sell the click** — bake in enough curiosity that the click-through rate
   beats every other advertiser on the platform, so Facebook rewards us with
   the lowest CPMs and best inventory

---

## The Audience Pyramid (use this to size message style)

In any given market:
- **3%** are looking to buy right now
- **17%** are in information-gathering mode
- **20%** are problem-aware but not actively shopping
- **60%** are not even problem-aware

Facebook lets us target the entire pyramid because we're pushing a message
in front of an audience whether they're thinking about it or not. Most ads
fail because they only speak to the 3%. Hyperdopamine ads work the whole
pyramid by articulating problems the audience didn't know they had.

## What Drives a Click

In rough order of power:

1. **Curiosity** (the most powerful — nothing trumps it)
2. **Intrigue**
3. **Shock**
4. **Direct benefit**
5. **Implied benefit** ("how is this of use to me?")
6. **Fear**
7. **Vanity**
8. **Self-interest** — better, richer, stronger, faster, healthier, happier,
   sexier, fitter, smarter

When in doubt, lead with **curiosity + a specific benefit.**

## The Anatomy of a Hyperdopamine Ad

Every ad needs ALL of these three working together:

1. **Pattern Interrupt** — image/visual that stops the scroll. Weird,
   surprising, or just visually unexpected.
2. **Burning Intrigue** — copy that creates a question the reader has to
   click to answer. "Why?" "How?" "What is that?"
3. **Big Specific Benefit** — what the targeted reader gets if they click,
   stated specifically (not "lose weight" → "lose 12-19 kilos in 5 weeks")

If only intrigue → blind clickbait, brings the wrong audience.
If only benefit → boring, gets ignored.
**The intersection of intrigue + targeted benefit = hyperdopamine.**

### The Three-Question Test

Before publishing any ad, check:
- Does it interrupt someone's pattern? (Is the image weird/native/unexpected?)
- Does it create burning intrigue in both creative AND headline?
- Does it promise a big specific benefit to a defined audience?

If all three answers aren't yes, rewrite.

## Ad Formats That Work (in order of Sabri's preference)

### 1. Raw Native (top performer)
A photo taken on an iPhone. Looks like organic content, not an ad. Multiple
people in frame performs especially well. The Facebook algorithm favors
content that looks native to the platform.

> "The first rule of advertising: do not make your ads look like ads."

### 2. Breaking News (top performer — works in every niche)
Frames the message as breaking news. Image of person looking surprised/
shocked, secondary image, headline written like a news headline. Sabri's
agency joke: "We break more news than the news channels combined."

⚠️ Don't overuse — don't make ALL ads breaking news. Mix with raw native.

### 3. SMS-Style
Mock screenshot of a text message conversation. Customize sender name and
message body to the offer.

### 4. Native Highlight
Native photo + secondary image + a red circle/arrow drawing attention to
something specific in the image. Looks scrappy, performs well.

### 5. Native Social Post
Mimics the format of high-engagement posts on Lad Bible, ENews, TMZ —
primary image + secondary image + curiosity headline.

### 6. Secret Info / Map
For location- or info-heavy offers (real estate, local services, hidden
data). Map + real photo + curiosity headline. Example: *"Property investment
revealed — this secret Victorian suburb is set to boom."*

---

## The Two-Image Pattern

Most top-performing ads use **a primary image + a secondary image**. Reason:
the brain has to process two visual elements to comprehend the ad, which
slows the scroll. That extra second is enough to read the headline.

When designing creative, default to two images unless there's a strong
reason for one.

## Headlines: Steal the Frame, Adapt the Specifics

Don't write headlines from a blank canvas. Look at the most-engaged content
on the platform and **adapt the frame to your niche.** Examples from the
transcript, with the adaptation method:

| Source headline (gossip/news) | Adapted to a Bernie project |
|---|---|
| "Taylor Swift fans go wild after spotting X-rated detail" | "Aussies going wild after spotting home loan hack" |
| "Insane new documentary leaves viewers screaming" | "Insane new mindset trick leaves anxiety sufferers speechless" |
| "True story behind twisted Netflix documentary" | "True story behind a 56kg weight loss journey" |
| "iPhone user advised to do one thing to stop battery drain" | "CEOs advised to do one thing before going home to family" |
| "Secret way to find out who called you" | "Secret way to find $5M in business funding without banks" |

**The lesson:** the news/gossip frame is the proven attention vehicle.
Channel the frame; populate it with your specific offer.

---

## Headline Patterns That Stop the Scroll

**Pattern A — Direct curiosity-driven claim**
> "This One Strategy Generated $1 Million in Sales in Just 30 Days"
> "This Single Insurance Mistake Cost a Montclair Family $42,000"

**Pattern B — Pattern-interrupt question**
> "Why Do Some Realtors Sell Homes 30% Faster in Monmouth County?"
> "Are You Tired of Marketing That Doesn't Work? Here's What You're Missing"

**Pattern C — Specific surprising statistic**
> "97% of NJ Homeowners Have a Coverage Gap They Don't Know About"
> "The Average HVAC Company Loses 12 Service Calls a Week to Bad Dispatch"

**Pattern D — Direct outcome with intrigue**
> "Get 4 New Clients This Week With This Tesla-Style Funnel"
> "Try On 89 Hairstyles Before You Sit in a Salon Chair"

**Pattern E — "Feels illegal" / forbidden curiosity**
> "This Client-Getting Hack Feels Illegal"
> "Realtors Hate This One Listing Trick"

**Pattern F — Vulnerability (works hard on TikTok / organic-feel ads)**
> "I'm terrified — I just shipped my first iOS app"
> "24 hours after launch, here's what I actually learned"

### Anti-Patterns — Never Write These

❌ "Revolutionize your [X] with unparalleled techniques"
❌ "At Cloud Insurance Agency, we believe..."
❌ "Comprehensive [X] solutions for the modern [Y]"
❌ "Looking for great insurance?"
❌ Brand-first openers, marketing-speak, and three-syllable buzzword stacks

## The Slippery Lead-In Copy (the most overlooked element)

After the headline and image, the **first line of the body copy** is the
single most under-optimized part of most ads. Sabri's agency has 5x'd
ROAS and dropped CPL by 5x by changing nothing else.

Job of the lead-in: **stop them again** and pull them into the body.
Treat it as a second hook — pattern-interrupt, intrigue, or a sharp
specific claim. Don't waste it on "Hey there!" or "Are you a business
owner who...".

---

## The Intriguing Link Description

The text under the headline (link description, on Facebook) is also
underused. Don't write "Learn more about our services." Write something
like *"Watch carefully then click."* It should add intrigue, not summarize.

---

## Body Copy: The Butter

**Length:** Aim for ~2,200 characters max (Facebook's limit for cross-running
on Instagram). Long copy outperforms short copy *if it's interesting.* Short
boring copy still loses.

**Process:** Write it without watching the count, then aggressively edit
down. Don't pad to fill.

**Reading level:** Run finished copy through the [Hemingway App](https://hemingwayapp.com).
Target a **3rd-4th grade reading level.** Bestselling NYT books, top YouTube
channels, all of them write at 5th grade or below. The easier it is to read,
the more people read it.

> "You don't want people to notice your writing. You want them to be moved
> by your message."

**Sentence and paragraph rules:**
- One- and two-syllable words wherever possible
- Short sentences. Like this. Single-line paragraphs are fine.
- Use lots of line breaks — 80-90% of readers are on mobile, walls of text
  feel like work
- Write like you talk
- "You" → "you" (write to one person, not a group)
- "We/us," not "King Kong" (don't refer to the brand in third person — talk
  human-to-human)

## Specificity = Believability

Generalized claims trigger skepticism. Specific claims build trust.

| Vague | Specific |
|---|---|
| "Lose weight fast" | "Lose 12-19 kilos in the next 5 weeks" |
| "Get more leads" | "Generate 90-480 inbound leads every month" |
| "Save on energy" | "Cut your power bill by 31% before winter" |
| "Improve your dispatching" | "Recover 12 service calls a week you're losing right now" |

The more specific the number, the more believable the claim.

---

## Theme Everything Around the Winning Concept

Once a hook starts working: **stop chasing new ideas. Refine and retest the
winner.** Sabri's words: "find a winner, theme everything around it, fully
send that one angle."

**This means:**
- Headline, image, lead-in, body, and CTA all reinforce ONE concept
- Don't run 5 unrelated angles in one ad — pick one and dial it in
- New variants test the SAME concept with small changes (headline tweak,
  image swap), not a totally different angle
- Once you've validated a winner, **scale it** before exploring alternatives

---

## CTA: Use "Learn More"

Sabri's agency split-tested every CTA option. **"Learn More" wins.** As of
the framework's current state, do not use "Buy Now," "Inquire Now," "Sign
Up," etc. for top-of-funnel ads. Save direct CTAs for retargeting.

---

## Positive vs Negative Framing — Big Benefit Wins ~8/10

Even in pain-heavy markets, **promising the big positive benefit out-pulls
avoiding the pain about 80% of the time.**

| Negative angle | Positive angle (usually wins) |
|---|---|
| "Don't put on 50 kg this winter" | "Lose 20-50 kg with this one weird hack" |
| "Stop losing service calls" | "Recover 12 service calls a week you didn't know you were losing" |
| "Don't fall behind on coverage" | "Sleep through the next storm — here's how" |

Test both, but lead with positive unless data says otherwise.

---

## Avoid Calling Out Individuals (Platform Compliance)

Facebook's policy: don't address users with "you" / "your" repeatedly in
ad copy. The platforms read "Are you struggling with X?" as calling out
the user's situation, which violates their content policy on personal
attributes (especially around health, finance, relationship status).

**Workaround:** speak about a third party or the general situation.
- ❌ "Are you tired of being overweight?"
- ✅ "Most people over 40 don't know about this metabolism trick"

This rule shifts over time — re-check current Meta policy when stakes are
high (health, financial, weight-loss claims).

## The Ad-Building Workflow (for any project)

When asked to produce marketing creative for any Bernie project, follow
this workflow:

### Step 1 — Read the project's CLAUDE.md first
Pull dream-buyer details, brand voice, color palette, claims-allowed list,
and any project-specific gotchas. Examples:
- Cloud Insurance: 20 named Essex County towns, 4-scenario pattern, color
  palette `#0A7DC2`/`#085A8D`/`#FFC107`, Verizon SMS gateway
- Chris Burns: Monmouth/Ocean/Middlesex counties, gold/navy palette, 973
  number (NEVER the 800 number)
- ZeroSugar: micro-influencer flat-fee model, no exaggerated user counts,
  AHA-cited science only
- CloudPriceDeals: dark theme `#FF6B35` orange accents, Amazon Associates
  disclosure required

### Step 2 — Define the dream buyer specifically
Don't write until you can answer:
1. Who specifically? (not "homeowners" — "Essex County homeowners with
   finished basements who lie awake during heavy rain")
2. Emotional pain (not "lacks coverage" — "afraid the next storm wipes them out")
3. Desired end state ("sleep through the next storm")
4. What they've already tried that didn't work
5. Their secret skepticism ("insurance companies are scams")

If the dream buyer isn't clear from CLAUDE.md, ask Craig before writing.

### Step 3 — Choose a format
Default to **raw native** or **breaking news** unless the project context
suggests otherwise. Pick from the format list above.

### Step 4 — Write the headline
- Pick a pattern (A-F above)
- Steal a frame from current high-engagement news/gossip if stuck
- Test against the three-question test (pattern interrupt, burning intrigue,
  big specific benefit)

### Step 5 — Brief the creative
Describe the image needed using the pattern-interrupt criteria:
- Native or breaking-news framing
- Two-image structure (primary + secondary)
- Specific visual element that creates intrigue
- Note any project-required brand elements (logo, colors, model

### Step 6 — Write slippery lead-in (1-2 sentences)
Treat it as a second hook. Pattern-interrupt or sharp specific claim.
Never waste it on "Hi there!" or generic openers.

### Step 7 — Write the butter body copy
- Target ~2,000 characters
- 3rd-4th grade reading level (run through Hemingway mentally)
- Single-line paragraphs, lots of line breaks
- Specific numbers everywhere
- One- and two-syllable words

### Step 8 — Intriguing link description + Learn More CTA
Don't summarize — add intrigue.

### Step 9 — Generate variants only by changing ONE element
- 3 headline variants (same concept)
- 3 image variants (same concept)
- 3 lead-in variants (same concept)
- Theme stays consistent across the whole set

### Step 10 — Output a creative brief
Hand back to Craig as a structured brief he can take into Meta Ads Manager:
- Headline (with 2-3 variants)
- Primary image description
- Secondary image description
- Lead-in copy
- Body copy
- Link description
- CTA: "Learn More"
- Suggested audience (if known from project context)
- Notes on what's being tested

## Cross-Project Adaptations (quick reference)

**CloudPriceDeals** — Frame deals as "findings." Sample: *"This $79 Amazon
deal has saved Jersey shoppers $34/order on average. The catch? It runs out
in 6 hours."* Lead with specificity (price + savings + time).

**Cloud Insurance** — Use Essex County town names + claim cost specificity.
Sample: *"This One Insurance Mistake Cost a Montclair Family $42,000."*
Frame: breaking news. Lead-in: scenario from the page.

**Chris Burns Realtor** — Lean local, lean specific. Sample: *"Why Some
Monmouth County Homes Sell in 11 Days While Others Sit for 6 Months."*
Frame: insider knowledge. Avoid generic realtor copy.

**PollPulse** — Vulnerability + indie-builder framing on TikTok. Sample:
*"Day 3 — I built a polling app and the users are arguing in the comments."*
Frame: behind-the-scenes builder.

**WigOut** — Visual transformation framing. Sample: *"She tried 12
hairstyles in 4 minutes — without sitting in a salon chair once."* Frame:
secret info or before/after. (Coordinate with Menelik before any messaging
or branding shift — partner-coordination per `wigout/CLAUDE.md`.)

**ZeroSugar Club** — Science-cited, AHA-anchored, no exaggerated user
counts. Sample: *"American Heart Association says women should eat 25g of
sugar a day. Most are eating 4x that — without knowing it."* Frame: news.

**Cloud Dispatch Ops** — Operations-pain framing for HVAC/plumbing owners.
Sample: *"The Average HVAC Shop Loses 12 Service Calls a Week to Bad
Dispatch — Here's How to See If You're One of Them."* Frame: industry
research.

**Cloud Design Studio** — Agency-credibility framing. Sample: *"How a
Branchburg, NJ Studio Built 7 Apps and Web Properties in 18 Months on a
Solo Budget."* Frame: case study / behind-the-scenes.

## When in Doubt

The whole framework collapses to one rule:

> **Make the ad look like content people would share.**
> News, gossip, findings, value — not corporate brand-speak.
> Stop the scroll. Pay off the click.

Everything else in this skill serves that one rule.
